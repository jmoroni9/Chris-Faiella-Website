Why Truck Cases Are Different

Trucking Cases Are Not Just Big Car Accident Cases

A tractor-trailer crash involves federal regulations, multiple potential defendants, complex insurance structures, and evidence that disappears fast. The moment a serious crash happens, the carrier's insurance company deploys adjusters and attorneys. You need someone equally prepared.

Federal Regulations (FMCSA)

Commercial trucking is governed by the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration. Hours-of-service rules, driver qualification requirements, vehicle maintenance standards — violations of these regulations are powerful evidence of negligence.

Electronic Evidence That Disappears

Modern trucks generate enormous amounts of electronic data — black box (ECM) data, GPS records, ELD logs, dashcam footage. This evidence can be overwritten quickly. Immediate legal action to preserve it is essential.

Multiple Defendants

Liability in truck accident cases often extends beyond the driver — to the carrier, the shipper, the loader, the truck manufacturer, or the maintenance company. Identifying all responsible parties maximizes your recovery.

High-Value Insurance Policies

Commercial carriers are required to carry significant liability coverage — often $1 million or more. This means more at stake, more aggressive defense, and the need for equally aggressive representation.

Federal Law & Your Case

FMCSA Violations That Strengthen Your Claim

The Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration establishes minimum safety standards for commercial trucks. When a carrier or driver violates these standards and a crash results, those violations are powerful evidence of negligence. Common violations we investigate include:

  • Hours-of-service violations — driving beyond legal limits
  • Falsified logbooks or ELD manipulation
  • Driver fatigue and drowsy driving
  • Failure to conduct proper pre-trip inspections
  • Brake and equipment maintenance failures
  • Improper load securement
  • Hiring unqualified or improperly licensed drivers
  • Failure to conduct drug and alcohol testing
  • Speeding, especially in adverse conditions
  • Overloaded or overweight vehicles
  • Inadequate driver training
  • Failure to maintain records of duty status

Time Is Critical in Truck Accident Cases

Trucking companies are required to retain certain records for defined periods — but some records can be destroyed after as little as 6 months. Driver logs, inspection records, maintenance files, and electronic data must be preserved through a litigation hold letter sent immediately. If you've been injured in a truck accident, contact an attorney as soon as possible.

Identifying All Responsible Parties

Who May Be Liable in a Truck Accident

One of the most important things a truck accident attorney does is identify all parties whose negligence contributed to the crash. Missouri law allows recovery from every responsible defendant.

🚛 The Truck Driver

Driver negligence — fatigue, distraction, impairment, speeding, improper lane changes — is often a primary cause. Drivers have personal liability even when employed by a carrier.

🏢 The Trucking Company

Carriers are vicariously liable for their drivers' negligence, and directly liable for their own negligence in hiring, training, supervising, and dispatching drivers.

📦 The Shipper / Loader

When improperly loaded or secured cargo contributes to a crash, the party responsible for loading may share liability — even if they are a separate company.

🔧 Maintenance Providers

Third-party mechanics or maintenance companies that negligently serviced the truck — especially brakes, tires, or steering — may bear responsibility for equipment failures.

🏭 The Truck Manufacturer

Defective truck components — defective brakes, tires, coupling systems — may give rise to products liability claims against the manufacturer or distributor.

🏗️ Cargo Owners

In some cases, the owner of the cargo being transported bears responsibility for how it was loaded or secured, or for pressuring the driver or carrier to violate safety rules.

Common Questions

Truck Accident FAQ

How are truck accident cases different from regular car accident cases?

Truck accident cases involve federal regulations (FMCSA), multiple potential defendants, complex insurance structures, and critical electronic evidence that must be preserved immediately. Carriers deploy professional accident response teams the moment a serious crash is reported. You need an attorney who is equally prepared and who acts quickly.

What is the statute of limitations for a truck accident in Missouri?

Missouri's general personal injury statute of limitations is five years. However, claims involving government entities have shorter deadlines, and the time-sensitive nature of electronic evidence means you should consult an attorney as soon as possible — ideally within days of the crash.

Can I sue the trucking company directly, not just the driver?

Yes. In most cases you can pursue claims against both the driver and the carrier. Carriers are vicariously liable for their employees' negligence under the doctrine of respondeat superior, and they may also be directly liable for their own negligent hiring, training, or supervision of the driver.

What if the truck driver was an independent contractor?

Carriers often attempt to classify drivers as independent contractors to avoid liability. Missouri courts look at the actual relationship — not just the label — and in many cases the carrier remains legally responsible. This is a fact-specific inquiry that requires careful investigation.
