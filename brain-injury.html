Understanding Brain Injury Claims

Why TBI Cases Require Specialized Legal Knowledge

Traumatic brain injuries don't always look dramatic on the outside. A person can walk away from a car accident looking uninjured — and be suffering from a brain injury that affects memory, concentration, personality, and the ability to work for years to come.

Insurance companies know this, and they exploit it. They point to normal CT scans and emergency room records that say "no loss of consciousness" to argue that nothing is wrong. They hire defense experts who minimize TBI symptoms. They argue the injured person was "fine" at the scene.

Winning a TBI case requires understanding the medicine deeply enough to confront those tactics — and having the right experts to explain the injury to a judge and jury.

Critical Point

A Normal CT Scan Does Not Mean No Brain Injury

Standard CT scans detect bleeding and obvious structural damage — but they cannot detect the diffuse axonal injury and microscopic shearing that causes the cognitive and behavioral symptoms of many TBIs. MRI, neuropsychological testing, and expert clinical evaluation are often necessary to fully document a brain injury.

If you have been told your scan was "normal" but you are experiencing headaches, memory problems, personality changes, or difficulty concentrating — those are TBI symptoms that deserve proper evaluation.

Types of Brain Injury

The Spectrum of Traumatic Brain Injury

Brain injuries range from mild concussion to severe, life-altering trauma. All levels of TBI can have significant consequences. Chris Faiella handles cases across the full spectrum.

Concussion (Mild TBI)

Even a "mild" TBI can have lasting consequences. Concussion symptoms — headaches, cognitive fog, light sensitivity, sleep disruption, irritability — can persist for months or years. Insurance companies routinely minimize these injuries; skilled legal and medical advocacy is essential.

Moderate TBI

Moderate TBI involves loss of consciousness, post-traumatic amnesia, and measurable cognitive impairment. These injuries frequently require hospitalization, rehabilitation, and can cause permanent changes to cognitive function, personality, and emotional regulation.

Severe TBI

Severe TBI may involve prolonged unconsciousness, coma, and profound, permanent disability. These cases involve enormous lifetime care costs and lost earning capacity. Maximum compensation requires extensive economic analysis and life-care planning.

Diffuse Axonal Injury

Caused by rotational forces in a crash, DAI involves widespread tearing of axons throughout the brain. It may not be visible on a standard CT scan but can cause devastating cognitive and motor deficits. Expert neurological testimony is critical in these cases.

What to Expect from the Insurance Company

How Insurers Fight TBI Claims

Understanding the defense playbook is the first step to defeating it. Here are the tactics insurance companies use to deny or minimize traumatic brain injury claims — and how we respond.

"The CT Scan Was Normal"

Standard CT scans do not detect diffuse axonal injury or many forms of TBI. We counter with MRI, neuropsychological testing, and expert neurological testimony that explains what the CT scan cannot see.

"There Was No Loss of Consciousness"

Loss of consciousness is not required for a TBI diagnosis. Disorientation, confusion, and altered awareness at the scene are also diagnostic criteria. We document these facts through witness accounts and first responder records.

"Your Client Seemed Fine After the Crash"

Many TBI symptoms are invisible — and some are delayed. Behavioral and cognitive changes are often first noticed by family members, not emergency responders. We build detailed lay witness testimony to document these changes.

"Pre-Existing Conditions"

Insurers claim that headaches, depression, or cognitive problems are pre-existing. Missouri's "eggshell plaintiff" rule holds defendants fully liable for aggravation of pre-existing conditions. We use medical records to isolate the crash's impact.

"Low-Speed Impact Cannot Cause TBI"

Research shows that brain injury can occur at speeds as low as 15 mph (6.7 m/s). We retain biomechanical engineers and accident reconstructionists to address speed, delta-V, and the forces involved in the specific crash.

"You're Exaggerating Your Symptoms"

Insurers hire psychologists to administer symptom validity testing and argue malingering. We prepare our clients, retain our own neuropsychologists, and challenge the defense experts' methodology and conclusions.

Common Questions

Brain Injury FAQ

Can I have a brain injury if my scans came back normal?

Yes. Standard CT scans cannot detect diffuse axonal injury or many forms of TBI that cause significant symptoms. If you are experiencing headaches, cognitive problems, memory issues, sensitivity to light or sound, mood changes, or sleep disruption after an accident, these are potential TBI symptoms that warrant a full neurological evaluation — regardless of your CT scan results.

How long after an accident can TBI symptoms appear?

Some TBI symptoms are immediate; others develop or worsen over days, weeks, or even months following the injury. Post-concussion syndrome, for example, can persist for a year or longer. Delayed symptoms do not mean the injury was not caused by the accident — it simply means the full picture took time to develop.

What is the value of a traumatic brain injury case?

TBI cases vary enormously in value depending on the severity of the injury, the extent of cognitive and functional impairment, the impact on earning capacity, and the lifetime cost of care. Severe TBI cases can have multi-million-dollar valuations when lifetime care costs and lost earnings are fully calculated. A free case evaluation can give you a realistic picture of your specific situation.

What if the insurer says the crash was too minor to cause a brain injury?

This is one of the most common defense arguments in TBI cases. The biomechanical research does not support a simple correlation between vehicle damage and brain injury. Studies show that TBI can occur at speeds as low as 15 mph. We retain biomechanical engineers and accident reconstructionists to address this argument directly with scientific evidence.
