What Medical Malpractice Really Means

When a Healthcare Provider Fails You

Medical malpractice occurs when a doctor, nurse, surgeon, hospital, or other healthcare provider fails to meet the accepted standard of care in their field — and that failure causes serious injury or death to a patient.

Not every bad medical outcome is malpractice. Medicine involves risk, and not all complications are the result of negligence. But when a provider's conduct falls below what a reasonably competent professional would have done under the same circumstances — and the patient suffers serious harm as a result — there is a case.

Medical malpractice cases are among the most complex in personal injury law. They require specialized medical experts, rigorous analysis of records, and an attorney willing to take on hospitals and their insurers. Chris Faiella accepts only serious cases where the evidence of negligence and the extent of harm justify the investment required to win.

The Legal Standard

What Is the Standard of Care?

The "standard of care" is what a reasonably competent healthcare provider in the same specialty would have done under the same or similar circumstances. It is not perfection — medicine is not perfect. But it is the baseline of competent, accepted practice.

Proving a deviation from the standard of care requires expert medical testimony. Missouri law requires that a plaintiff in a medical malpractice case obtain a written opinion from a qualified medical expert before filing suit, confirming that the claim has merit.

Types of Medical Malpractice

Cases We Handle

Medical negligence takes many forms. The cases Chris Faiella accepts share two things: clear evidence that a provider fell below the standard of care, and serious, life-altering harm to the patient.

🤰 Birth Injuries

Oxygen deprivation, improper use of forceps or vacuum, failure to perform a timely C-section, and other obstetric negligence can cause cerebral palsy, brain injury, and permanent disability in newborns.

⚕️ Surgical Errors

Wrong-site surgery, perforated organs, retained surgical instruments, anesthesia errors, and post-operative negligence. Surgical malpractice can cause catastrophic, permanent harm.

🔍 Misdiagnosis & Delayed Diagnosis

Failure to diagnose cancer, heart attack, stroke, pulmonary embolism, or serious infection in time. When a timely diagnosis would have changed the outcome, the delay is actionable.

💊 Medication Errors

Wrong drug, wrong dose, dangerous drug interactions, or failure to account for known allergies. Medication errors occur in hospitals, pharmacies, and outpatient settings and can be deadly.

🏥 Hospital Negligence

Inadequate nursing care, failure to monitor, hospital-acquired infections from inadequate protocols, falls due to insufficient precautions, and negligent credentialing of staff physicians.

🫀 Failure to Treat

Premature discharge, failure to order indicated tests, failure to refer to a specialist, and failure to act on abnormal test results. What a provider failed to do can be just as actionable as what they did wrong.

🧠 Anesthesia Errors

Too much or too little anesthesia, failure to review patient history for contraindications, improper intubation, and inadequate monitoring during surgery can cause brain damage, cardiac arrest, or death.

🏡 Nursing Home Abuse & Neglect

Residents of nursing homes and assisted living facilities are among the most vulnerable patients. Pressure sores, falls, medication errors, physical abuse, and neglect of basic care needs can rise to the level of medical malpractice and may support additional elder abuse claims.

⚰️ Wrongful Death

When medical negligence claims a life, the surviving family may pursue a wrongful death claim. These cases can include end-of-life suffering, loss of the decedent's earnings, and the family's loss of companionship and support.

What Makes These Cases Difficult

Why Medical Malpractice Cases Require Specialized Counsel

Medical malpractice litigation is among the most demanding in all of personal injury law. Understanding the challenges is the first step to overcoming them.

Expert Requirements

Missouri requires a qualified medical expert's written affidavit before a malpractice suit can be filed. At trial, every element of the case — standard of care, deviation, causation — must be proven through expert testimony. Retaining the right experts, from the right specialties, is essential.

Causation Complexity

Proving that the provider's negligence — not the underlying illness or condition — caused the patient's harm is often the hardest part of a malpractice case. Defense experts will argue the patient's outcome would have been the same regardless of the negligence. Defeating that argument requires strong medical and expert analysis.

Well-Funded Defense

Hospitals and physicians carry substantial malpractice insurance. Their insurers retain experienced defense firms who handle nothing but malpractice defense. You need an attorney who is equally prepared, equally resourced, and willing to take the case to trial.

Missouri Damage Caps

Missouri imposes a cap on non-economic damages in medical malpractice cases. Understanding how the cap applies — and how to maximize economic damages, including future medical costs and lost earning capacity, which are not capped — is critical to full recovery.

An Honest Assessment

Most Calls Don't Become Cases — And That's Okay

Medical malpractice is one of the most misunderstood areas of law. Patients and families who have suffered a terrible outcome often believe — understandably — that something must have gone wrong. Sometimes it did. Often, what happened was a known risk of a procedure, a complication of age or underlying disease, or a difficult judgment call that reasonable physicians could disagree on.

The reality is that most malpractice inquiries are declined at the first call or first meeting — not because the outcome wasn't tragic, but because the law requires proof that the provider's conduct fell below the accepted standard of care, and that the deviation caused the harm. That is a high bar, and it should be.

Missouri Law

Key Missouri Rules in Medical Malpractice Cases

Missouri has specific procedural and substantive rules that govern medical malpractice claims. An experienced attorney navigates these requirements from day one.

Statute of Limitations

Missouri's deadline for medical malpractice claims is two years from the date the patient knew or should have known of the negligence — with one important exception: when the malpractice causes a death, the wrongful death claim carries a three-year statute of limitations.

The statute may be tolled in certain circumstances — for minors, fraudulent concealment, or other specific situations. But regardless of tolling arguments, acting quickly is always the right approach. Medical records get lost, witnesses' memories fade, and evidence that is available today may not be available a year from now. If you think something went wrong, call sooner rather than later.

Pre-Suit Expert Affidavit

Missouri law requires that a plaintiff in a medical malpractice case file a written affidavit from a legally qualified healthcare provider stating that the defendant failed to meet the standard of care. This requirement must be satisfied before suit is filed, and selecting the right expert for this purpose is an important early step.

Non-Economic Damage Cap

Missouri caps non-economic damages (pain and suffering, loss of consortium, emotional distress) in medical malpractice cases. The cap amount is adjusted periodically for inflation. Economic damages — medical expenses, lost income, future care costs — are not subject to a cap and can be substantial in serious injury cases.

Joint and Several Liability

Missouri follows a modified system of joint and several liability. When multiple defendants are responsible for your harm — such as a surgeon and a hospital — the rules governing how damages are apportioned among them require careful attention to maximize recovery from all available sources.

Common Questions

Medical Malpractice FAQ

How do I know if I have a medical malpractice case?

The key question is whether your healthcare provider's conduct fell below the accepted standard of care — and whether that failure caused your injury. A bad outcome alone is not malpractice; medicine involves inherent risks. The best way to evaluate your situation is a free consultation, where Chris Faiella will review the facts and give you an honest assessment of whether the case has merit worth pursuing.

How long do I have to file a medical malpractice claim in Missouri?

Generally two years from the date you knew or reasonably should have known about the negligence — with one exception: if the malpractice resulted in a death, the wrongful death claim has a three-year statute of limitations. The statute may be tolled in certain circumstances, including for minors and in cases of fraudulent concealment. But these deadlines are strict, and waiting to consult an attorney is always a risk — evidence and witnesses that are available today may not be available later. If you have concerns, call as soon as possible.

Are medical malpractice cases expensive to pursue?

Yes — they require significant investment in expert witnesses, medical record review, and litigation costs. Chris Faiella handles malpractice cases on a contingency fee basis, meaning you pay no attorney fees unless we win. Case costs are advanced by the firm and recovered at the end of a successful case. He accepts only cases where the strength of the evidence and the seriousness of the harm justify that investment.

Can I sue a hospital as well as the doctor?

Often yes. Hospitals may be directly liable for their own negligence — inadequate staffing, failure to maintain equipment, negligent credentialing — and may also be vicariously liable for the negligence of their employed physicians and staff. Identifying all liable defendants is one of the most important early steps in a malpractice case.

What if a family member died from medical negligence?

Missouri's wrongful death statute allows surviving family members — spouse, children, parents — to pursue a claim when a loved one dies as a result of medical malpractice. These cases can recover damages for the family's loss as well as the decedent's pre-death pain and suffering. The same two-year statute of limitations applies.
