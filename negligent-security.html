Holding Property Owners Accountable

When Inadequate Security Enables Violence

Negligent security is a form of premises liability — a property owner's failure to provide security measures adequate to protect visitors from foreseeable criminal acts. It applies to apartment complexes, hotels, parking lots, bars and nightclubs, shopping centers, hospitals, schools, and any other property where the owner knew or should have known that crime was a risk.

The key legal concept is foreseeability. A property owner is not an insurer of visitors' safety — but when prior criminal activity on or near the property made violence foreseeable, the owner has a duty to take reasonable steps to prevent it. Inadequate lighting, broken security cameras, unstaffed entry points, a history of unreported incidents — these failures create liability when someone is hurt.

These cases are often vigorously defended. Property owners and their insurers argue the criminal act was unforeseeable, or that adequate security was in place. Chris Faiella knows how to build the evidentiary record — crime reports, incident logs, security assessments — that establishes foreseeability and defeats those defenses.

The Legal Standard

Foreseeability Is the Key Question

Missouri courts hold property owners liable for third-party criminal acts when those acts were reasonably foreseeable given the totality of the circumstances — including prior criminal incidents on the property, the nature of the business, the location, and the owner's actual knowledge of security risks.

A single prior similar incident is not always required. A pattern of crime in the area, the known risks of a particular type of business, or actual knowledge of specific threats can all establish foreseeability.

Where These Cases Arise

Properties With a Duty to Provide Security

Negligent security cases arise anywhere a property owner invites the public onto their premises and has reason to know that crime is a foreseeable risk. Some locations carry heightened security obligations by the nature of the business.

🏢 Apartment Complexes

Landlords must maintain reasonably secure common areas, parking lots, entryways, and laundry facilities. A history of break-ins, assaults, or prior tenant complaints puts the landlord on notice of the need for adequate security measures.

🍺 Bars & Nightclubs

Establishments serving alcohol have heightened obligations to prevent violence on and immediately outside their premises. Inadequate staffing, failure to remove threatening patrons, and poor lighting in parking areas are common liability triggers.

🏨 Hotels & Motels

Hotel guests have a reasonable expectation of security in their rooms, hallways, parking lots, and common areas. Broken locks, propped doors, inadequate lighting, and unstaffed entry points have all been the basis of negligent security claims.

🛒 Shopping Centers & Retail

High-traffic retail properties in areas with known criminal activity must take reasonable steps to protect customers. Prior robberies, assaults, or carjackings in the parking lot put owners on notice of the security measures required.

🏫 Schools & Campuses

Schools, colleges, and universities have a duty to provide reasonably safe environments for students and faculty. Failures in access control, inadequate response protocols, and known threats that went unaddressed can support negligent security claims.

🅿️ Parking Lots & Garages

Parking facilities are among the most common locations for criminal attacks. Inadequate lighting, broken security cameras, lack of patrols, and poor visibility make parking structures dangerous — and their owners potentially liable.

Evidence That Establishes Liability

What Makes a Negligent Security Case

Building a successful negligent security case requires assembling evidence that the criminal act was foreseeable and that the property owner's security measures were inadequate. This is the evidentiary foundation of every case.

Prior Criminal Incidents

Police reports, incident logs, 911 call histories, and prior lawsuits involving the same property establish that crime was foreseeable. We obtain this evidence through public records requests and discovery.

Inadequate Security Measures

Broken or absent security cameras, non-functioning lighting, broken locks, lack of security personnel, and failure to follow the property owner's own security policies are all evidence of inadequate security.

Security Industry Standards

We retain security industry experts who can testify about what reasonable security measures are required for a given type of property — and how the defendant's measures fell short of that standard.

The Owner's Own Knowledge

Internal emails, maintenance records, security audits, and complaints from tenants or customers that the owner received but ignored are powerful evidence that the owner knew about the security problem and chose not to address it.

Common Questions

Negligent Security FAQ

Can I sue a property owner if I was attacked by a third party on their property?

Yes — if the criminal act was foreseeable and the property owner failed to take reasonable security measures to prevent it. The fact that the immediate cause of harm was a criminal act does not insulate the property owner from liability if their negligence enabled the attack. Missouri courts have consistently held property owners liable in these circumstances.

What if there had never been a crime at that property before?

Prior incidents at the exact same location are strong evidence of foreseeability but are not always required. Crime statistics for the surrounding area, the nature of the business (a late-night bar, for example), and industry-wide knowledge of security risks can all establish foreseeability even without a prior incident at that specific address.

Does it matter that the attacker was a criminal?

No. The criminal acts of a third party do not break the chain of liability when the property owner's negligence was a contributing cause of the harm. Missouri law holds that property owners can be held liable even when the immediate cause of injury was a third party's intentional criminal act — as long as that act was foreseeable.

How long do I have to file a negligent security claim in Missouri?

Missouri's statute of limitations for negligent security claims is generally five years from the date of the injury. If the property is owned by a government entity, there are significantly shorter notice requirements. Contact an attorney promptly — evidence including security footage and incident logs may be overwritten or destroyed quickly.
