What Is a Wrongful Death Claim

When the Law Gives a Family a Voice

A wrongful death claim is a civil lawsuit brought by surviving family members when a person is killed by the negligent, reckless, or intentional conduct of another. It is separate from any criminal prosecution — a family can pursue a wrongful death claim even if no criminal charges are filed, and even if criminal charges result in an acquittal.

Missouri's wrongful death statute, RSMo § 537.080, gives the family a right to recover for the losses the death caused — both the financial losses and the profound human ones. The goal is not just compensation. It is accountability. It is making the responsible party answer in court for what they did.

Chris Faiella understands that these are the hardest cases a family will ever face. He handles wrongful death claims with the gravity they deserve — and with the determination to see them through.

A Note on Timing

Missouri's wrongful death statute provides a three-year statute of limitations — compared to five years for most personal injury claims. But waiting is never advisable. Witnesses' recollections fade, physical evidence disappears, and critical records may become unavailable. The sooner a family consults an attorney, the stronger the case that can be built.

How These Cases Arise

Common Causes of Wrongful Death Claims

A wrongful death claim can arise from any situation where negligence, recklessness, or intentional conduct causes a death. Chris Faiella handles wrongful death cases arising from a wide range of circumstances.

🚗 Car & Truck Accidents

Motor vehicle crashes are among the most common causes of wrongful death. Distracted driving, drunk driving, speeding, and commercial carrier negligence all give rise to wrongful death claims.

🚛 Tractor-Trailer Crashes

Commercial truck accidents frequently result in fatalities. These cases involve federal regulations, multiple defendants, and carriers with substantial insurance — and require immediate action to preserve evidence.

🏍️ Motorcycle Collisions

Motorcyclists are among the most vulnerable people on the road. When a driver's negligence causes a fatal motorcycle crash, the family has a wrongful death claim against the at-fault driver and, where applicable, their employer.

🏠 Premises Liability

Property owners have a duty to maintain safe conditions. Negligent security, dangerous conditions, inadequate maintenance, and failure to warn can all result in fatal injuries — and wrongful death liability — for property owners and occupiers.

⚕️ Medical Malpractice

When a healthcare provider's negligence causes a patient's death, the family may pursue both a wrongful death claim and a survival action. The statute of limitations in these cases is three years.

🏭 Products Liability

Defective vehicles, machinery, consumer products, and medical devices can cause fatal injuries. Manufacturers and distributors are strictly liable for deaths caused by defective products they put into the stream of commerce.

🚤 Boating & Jet Ski Collisions

Negligent boat and personal watercraft operators cause serious and fatal injuries on Missouri's lakes and rivers. These cases may involve federal maritime law, state boating regulations, and liability of vessel owners and operators.

🔥 Fires & Explosions

Fatal fires and explosions can result from defective products, negligent property maintenance, gas line failures, or workplace safety violations. Multiple defendants — manufacturers, landlords, contractors — may share liability.

🏊 Drowning & Water Accidents

Drowning deaths at pools, lakes, and recreational facilities often involve negligent supervision, inadequate safety measures, or defective equipment. Property owners and operators have a duty to maintain safe conditions.

🏕️ ATV & Off-Road Crashes

Fatal ATV and off-road vehicle accidents may involve driver negligence, defective vehicle design or manufacturing, or hazardous property conditions. These cases often implicate both personal injury and products liability theories.

🏈 School & Sports Injuries

Schools, coaches, and sports organizations have a duty to provide reasonably safe conditions and supervision. When that duty is breached and a student or athlete suffers a fatal injury, the institution and its responsible personnel may be held accountable.

🏗️ Workplace Accidents

When a workplace death is caused by a third party's negligence — a contractor, equipment manufacturer, or property owner — a wrongful death claim may be pursued in addition to workers' compensation benefits.

Missouri Wrongful Death Law

Who Can Bring a Wrongful Death Claim in Missouri

Missouri's wrongful death statute establishes a specific order of priority for who may bring a claim. Understanding this structure matters — only certain family members have the right to sue, and the damages are distributed according to the statute.

Class I — Spouse, Children & Grandchildren

The first priority class under RSMo § 537.080 consists of the deceased's spouse, children, and — in some circumstances — grandchildren. If any Class I claimant exists, they have the exclusive right to bring the wrongful death action. The recovery is for their benefit.

Class II — Parents & Siblings

If there is no surviving spouse, child, or grandchild, the right to bring a wrongful death claim passes to the deceased's parents and siblings. This class most often applies in the tragic death of a young adult or child with no spouse or children of their own.

Plaintiff Ad Litem

If no Class I or Class II claimant exists or is able to bring the action, the court may appoint a plaintiff ad litem — typically another family member or interested person — to pursue the claim on behalf of the estate and any remaining beneficiaries.

One Action Rule

Missouri law permits only one wrongful death action per death. All beneficiaries in the same class must be included in or bound by that single action. This means early coordination among family members — and with counsel — is important to protect everyone's interests.

What the Family May Recover

Damages in a Missouri Wrongful Death Case

Missouri's wrongful death statute allows recovery for both the economic losses the death caused and the profound non-economic losses that no amount of money can truly replace. A full recovery requires calculating both.

Economic Damages

  • Lost earnings the deceased would have provided over their lifetime
  • Lost benefits — pension, Social Security, health insurance
  • Medical and hospital expenses incurred before death
  • Funeral and burial expenses
  • Value of services the deceased provided to the family
  • Lost financial support and contributions to the household

Non-Economic Damages

  • The grief, bereavement, and mental anguish of surviving family members
  • Loss of the deceased's companionship, comfort, and guidance
  • Loss of consortium for a surviving spouse
  • Loss of parental guidance and support for surviving children
  • The deceased's pre-death pain and suffering (survival action)
  • Punitive damages in cases involving egregious or reckless conduct

Common Questions

Wrongful Death FAQ

How is a wrongful death claim different from a criminal case?

A wrongful death claim is a civil lawsuit — it is brought by the family, not by the government, and it seeks financial compensation rather than criminal punishment. The standard of proof is lower than in a criminal case: preponderance of the evidence (more likely than not) rather than beyond a reasonable doubt. A family can bring a wrongful death claim regardless of whether criminal charges are filed, and a not-guilty verdict in a criminal case does not prevent a successful civil claim.

How long do we have to file a wrongful death claim in Missouri?

Missouri's wrongful death statute provides a three-year statute of limitations. By comparison, most personal injury claims in Missouri carry a five-year limit — so wrongful death claims actually have a shorter window. Acting quickly is always advisable regardless of the deadline. Evidence can disappear, witnesses' memories fade, and records that are available today may not be available later. The sooner you consult an attorney, the better positioned your family will be.

Can we sue even if there was no criminal prosecution?

Yes. A civil wrongful death claim is entirely independent of the criminal justice system. Many wrongful death cases — car accidents, medical malpractice, defective products — never involve criminal charges at all. The absence of a criminal prosecution has no bearing on the viability of a civil claim.

What if our loved one was partly at fault?

Missouri follows a pure comparative fault rule, which means a wrongful death claim can succeed even if the deceased shared some responsibility for what happened. The family's recovery is reduced proportionally by the deceased's percentage of fault — but it is not eliminated. Don't assume a claim isn't viable because the situation was complicated.

How is the recovery divided among family members?

Missouri law requires that the recovery in a wrongful death case be distributed among the beneficiaries in proportion to the damages each has suffered. The court must approve the distribution. In cases with multiple family members — a spouse and children, for example — early coordination with counsel helps ensure the process goes smoothly and everyone's interests are protected.
