What Is Insurance Bad Faith?

When Insurers Cross the Line

Insurance companies have a legal duty to deal fairly and in good faith with their policyholders and, in the third-party context, with claimants. When an insurer violates that duty — through unreasonable claim denial, failure to investigate, inadequate settlement offers, or refusal to defend — the law holds them accountable.

Missouri has one of the strongest statutory bad faith regimes in the country. RSMo § 375.420 allows an insured to recover the full amount of the judgment against them — even in excess of policy limits — plus attorney fees and a penalty, when an insurer vexatiously refuses to pay a claim. On the third-party side, an insurer who fails to settle within policy limits when it reasonably should have may be liable for the full excess judgment.

These are high-stakes, complex cases. Chris Faiella has the experience, the legal knowledge, and the litigation record to handle them — and welcomes referrals from other attorneys.

Vexatious Refusal Under Missouri Law
RSMo § 375.420

Missouri's vexatious refusal statute allows a policyholder whose valid insurance claim has been wrongfully denied to recover not only the policy benefits owed, but also damages of up to 20% of the first $1,500 and 10% of the remainder of the claim, plus reasonable attorney fees.

The standard: whether the insurer's refusal was "without reasonable cause or excuse." This is a fact-intensive inquiry that rewards thorough preparation and aggressive advocacy.

Types of Insurance Bad Faith Claims

Missouri Insurance Bad Faith: The Landscape

Third-Party Bad Faith: Failure to Settle

When an insured faces a liability claim within policy limits and the insurer unreasonably refuses to settle, the insurer may be liable for the full amount of any excess judgment. This claim is typically assigned to the plaintiff's PI attorney as part of the underlying case resolution — and referred to insurance bad faith counsel for pursuit.

First-Party Vexatious Refusal (§ 375.420)

When an insurer wrongfully denies or delays payment of a first-party claim — UM/UIM benefits, property damage, homeowners, life insurance, disability — without reasonable cause, the insured may recover the withheld benefits plus statutory penalties and attorney fees under RSMo § 375.420.

Failure to Defend

An insurer who improperly refuses to defend its insured in a covered suit breaches its duty to defend and may be liable for all resulting damages, including any judgment entered against the insured. The duty to defend is broader than the duty to indemnify.

Improper Claims Handling

Inadequate investigation, misrepresentation of policy provisions, unreasonable delays, lowball offers without basis, and other unfair claims practices may support bad faith claims. Missouri's insurance regulations set minimum standards for claims handling that can be used as evidence of bad faith conduct.

The Missouri Framework

Missouri Law on Insurance Bad Faith

Missouri provides multiple legal theories for pursuing insurance companies that act in bad faith. Understanding the interplay among these theories — and which applies to a given set of facts — is essential to maximizing recovery.

RSMo § 375.420 — Vexatious Refusal

The primary first-party bad faith statute. Applies when an insurer refuses to pay a policy claim "without reasonable cause or excuse." Recovery includes the withheld benefits, up to 20% penalty on the first $1,500 and 10% on the remainder, and reasonable attorney fees.

Common Law Bad Faith — Failure to Settle

Missouri recognizes an insurer's duty to settle a third-party claim within policy limits when it is reasonably prudent to do so. Breach of this duty exposes the insurer to liability for the full excess judgment — with no cap at policy limits.

RSMo § 379.195 — Liability Fixed at Time of Loss

Once a covered loss occurs, the insurer's liability is fixed and cannot be extinguished by any subsequent agreement between the insurer and insured. This statute directly forecloses retroactive cancellation arguments after an accident has occurred.

Punitive Damages

In egregious cases where an insurer's conduct reflects a culpable mental state — conscious disregard for the rights of the insured — punitive damages may be available in addition to compensatory damages and statutory penalties.

Common Questions

Insurance Bad Faith FAQ

What is vexatious refusal under Missouri law?

Under RSMo § 375.420, an insurer commits "vexatious refusal" when it refuses to pay a valid policy claim without reasonable cause or excuse. The statute allows recovery of the withheld benefits, a penalty of up to 20% of the first $1,500 and 10% of the remainder, and reasonable attorney fees. The claim is fact-specific — what matters is whether the insurer had a legitimate, objectively reasonable basis for its denial.

What is third-party bad faith failure to settle?

When an insurer is defending its insured against a liability claim, the insurer has a duty to give equal consideration to the interests of the insured when evaluating settlement opportunities. If a plaintiff makes a policy-limits demand and the insurer unreasonably refuses to settle — and an excess verdict results — the insurer may be liable for the full amount of the judgment above policy limits. This claim is typically assigned by the insured to the plaintiff as part of the case resolution.

Can I bring a bad faith claim even if the insurer eventually paid?

Potentially. Unreasonable delay in paying a valid claim — even if ultimately paid — may support a vexatious refusal claim under § 375.420. The focus is on the insurer's conduct: was the refusal or delay without reasonable cause? If so, statutory penalties and attorney fees may be available even if the benefits were eventually paid.
