Property Owner Responsibility

The Duty to Maintain Safe Property

Premises liability is the body of law governing injuries that occur on someone else's property. It applies to businesses, landlords, homeowners, government entities, and anyone else who owns, occupies, or controls real property.

In Missouri, property owners owe a duty of reasonable care to people they invite onto their property — customers, tenants, guests, and others. That duty includes inspecting the property for dangerous conditions, fixing known hazards, and warning visitors of dangers that are not obvious.

When a property owner fails that duty and someone is seriously injured as a result, the owner may be held liable for the full extent of the injured person's damages. These cases require proving that the owner knew or should have known about the dangerous condition and failed to act.

What You Need to Prove

Elements of a Premises Liability Claim

To succeed in a Missouri premises liability case, you generally must show:

  • The defendant owned, occupied, or controlled the property
  • A dangerous condition existed on the property
  • The defendant knew or should have known about the condition
  • The defendant failed to repair, warn, or remedy the condition
  • The dangerous condition caused your injury
  • You suffered real, compensable damages
Common Types of Premises Liability Cases

How These Injuries Happen

Dangerous conditions on property take many forms. Chris Faiella handles serious premises liability cases across a wide range of circumstances — accepting cases where the injuries are significant and the evidence of negligence is clear.

🧊 Slip, Trip & Fall

Wet floors, icy walkways, uneven pavement, unmarked steps, broken flooring, and poor lighting cause thousands of serious fall injuries every year. Businesses and property owners who fail to address these conditions are liable for the consequences.

🏗️ Construction & Structural Defects

Collapsing structures, defective staircases, inadequate railings, falling objects, and construction site hazards. Both property owners and contractors may be liable when structural failures cause injury.

🔥 Fires & Explosions on Property

Property owners who fail to maintain fire safety systems, gas lines, or electrical systems — or who fail to address known fire hazards — may be liable when fires or explosions injure or kill people on their property.

🏊 Swimming Pool Accidents

Negligent pool maintenance, inadequate fencing and barriers, lack of supervision, and defective pool equipment. Pool owners — residential and commercial — have heightened duties to prevent drowning and serious injury.

🐕 Dog Bites & Animal Attacks

Missouri's dog bite statute imposes strict liability on dog owners for bites that occur when the victim was lawfully on the property. Serious dog attacks can cause disfiguring injuries, nerve damage, and lasting psychological trauma.

🏢 Elevator & Escalator Accidents

Commercial property owners are responsible for maintaining elevators and escalators in safe working condition. Malfunctions, entrapments, and falls caused by defective equipment can result in catastrophic injuries.

Missouri Law

The Duty of Care Depends on Who You Are

Missouri law historically categorized visitors as invitees, licensees, or trespassers — and the duty owed differed by category. While Missouri has moved toward a general reasonableness standard in many cases, the status of the injured person can still affect the analysis. Understanding where you fit matters.

Invitees

People invited onto property for the owner's business purposes — customers in a store, patrons of a restaurant, visitors to a commercial facility. Owners owe invitees the highest duty: reasonable care to inspect, discover, and remedy dangerous conditions.

Licensees

Social guests and others who enter with permission but not for the owner's business benefit. Owners must warn licensees of known dangers that the visitor is unlikely to discover on their own.

Children & Attractive Nuisance

Missouri's attractive nuisance doctrine imposes a heightened duty on property owners when conditions on the property — pools, trampolines, construction equipment — are likely to attract and endanger children who may not appreciate the risks.

If You Were Injured on Someone's Property

What to Do After a Premises Injury

  • Seek medical attention immediately — even if you feel okay
  • Report the incident to the property owner or manager and get a copy of any report
  • Photograph the dangerous condition before it is repaired or altered
  • Get the names and contact information of any witnesses
  • Do not sign any forms or give recorded statements to the property's insurer
  • Preserve any footwear or clothing you were wearing at the time
  • Contact an attorney before the property owner has a chance to fix the hazard and destroy the evidence
What You May Recover

Damages in Premises Liability Cases

  • All past and future medical expenses
  • Lost wages and future earning capacity
  • Pain and suffering — past and future
  • Permanent disability and disfigurement
  • Emotional distress and psychological injury
  • Loss of enjoyment of life
  • Wrongful death damages for surviving family members
Common Questions

Premises Liability FAQ

Can I sue if I slipped and fell in a store?

Yes, if the store knew or should have known about the dangerous condition that caused your fall and failed to address it. The key question is notice — did the store create the condition, or did it exist long enough that a reasonable inspection would have discovered it? This is often the central factual dispute in slip and fall cases.

What if I was partly at fault for my injury?

Missouri follows pure comparative fault — your recovery is reduced by your percentage of fault, but you can still recover even if you were partly responsible. If you were 30% at fault and your damages were $100,000, you recover $70,000. Don't assume you have no case because you share some responsibility.

How long do I have to file a premises liability claim in Missouri?

Missouri's statute of limitations for premises liability claims is generally five years from the date of injury. Claims against government entities — city or county owned property — have much shorter notice requirements, sometimes as little as 90 days. If a government entity's property was involved, contact an attorney immediately.

What if the property owner claims I was trespassing?

Even trespassers have some legal protections in Missouri — particularly children under the attractive nuisance doctrine. And property owners sometimes mischaracterize the status of visitors. If you were injured on property where you had any reason to believe you were welcome, your status deserves a careful legal analysis.
